Write! app review

Not too shabby on Windows, not what you want on macOS.

June 26, 2017Filed under Tech#app reviews#design#macos#windows#writingMarkdown source

As I‚Äôve noted in the past, I‚Äôm always on the lookout for top-notch writing environments. I was recently contacted by the team behind Write! and asked if I would take a look at and review their app, and I was happy to obliged. I tested the app out fairly thoroughly by doing what I normally do with my writing apps: putting together a blog post or the like. I‚Äôve written this review from start to finish in it, across my two Mac machines. I promised the authors an unbiased review, so here we go!

Overview

Write!1 describes itself as a distraction-free text editor. It enters the market in an interesting way: the Mac offerings here are numerous, varied, and excellent. Offerings on Windows are fewer and further between, and in my experience of much lower quality. Distraction-free text editors outside the world of programming text editors barely exist at all on Linux, as far as I can tell.2 Write! is cross-platform, targeting all three of these. And that, as we‚Äôll see, is the story of this particular app‚Äîfor good and for ill.

The Good

First, the good: the app seems to perform relatively well. Text entry, even on a fairly large document, is smooth and quick. (I imported the text of this ~7200-word paper to test it and it didn‚Äôt stutter a bit.) Especially given the time I‚Äôm going to spend on the not-so-good below, I want to take a moment to applaud the developers for getting that right. It‚Äôs one of the most important aspects of an app like this, and any number of apps I‚Äôve used just fall down on large documents. Everything I‚Äôve seen here makes it seem like Write! would handle much larger documents even than that paper with aplomb.

The app‚Äôs main writing area looks fairly nice, and the distraction-free/full-screen mode gets out of the way readily enough. The cloud sync that comes with the app is quick and seems reliable. I‚Äôve worked on this document across the two Macs I use, with no sync issues whatsoever. The writing area also has a (toggleable) overview of the document on the right, a la Sublime Text. To the left is a toggleable outline view, which lets you drill down into the structure of your document if you have multiple heading levels. And within the writing area itself, you can expand and collapse sections demarcated by headings.

In general, the experience of writing in the app is nice. Not amazing, but genuinely nice.

The Just Okay

There‚Äôs a bit of a delay before any open tabs are hidden in that fullscreen mode, but it‚Äôs otherwise fairly typical of most ‚Äúdistraction-free‚Äù writing environments in that regard. The colors chosen for the light and dark writing themes are fine, but not great. Much the same is true of the typography: it‚Äôs relatively pleasant, if bland. There are a number of built-in themes, but no apparent way to customize them to be more to your liking.

The app also features built-in autocomplete‚Äîbut I‚Äôm not really sure who the target audience is for auto-complete in this kind of environment. It‚Äôs not bad, per se, to have it, but it doesn‚Äôt add a lot of value for writing (as opposed to, say, programming), and I turned it off fairly quickly in the process of writing this review.

Publishing

The app includes some ‚Äúpublishing‚Äù tools. Currently it supports writing to either Write‚Äôs own site, or to Medium. Medium publishing is nice‚Äîit‚Äôs certainly the hip tool du jour‚Äîbut you‚Äôre out of luck if you use WordPress, much less something like Ghost.3

Publishing to Write! itself seems to be mostly a way of letting people see in-progress drafts. The links aren‚Äôt particularly friendly, and while they‚Äôd be easy enough to share to Facebook or Twitter or the like, they have serious downsides over any of the free blogging options out there for anything other than getting some early feedback‚Äîthere‚Äôs no organizational or navigational structure available, and for that matter nothing that even ties it to your name! At a minimum, Write! should clarify what this is for.

Business model

The business model here is a curious mix: they‚Äôre selling the app at $19.95 (USD), with a year included of their custom sync solution. That sync solution is one of the things they advertise most heavily, and while I can attest that it works well, adding another, bespoke sync solution to my life is not on my list of things I‚Äôd like to do. It‚Äôs particularly an issue from where I stand because it doesn‚Äôt actually get me any benefits over a syncing solution using Dropbox or iCloud, both of which I‚Äôve used extensively with other writing apps in the last few years, with no issues.

Add onto that the fact that the sync and future updates become an annual purchase‚Äî

Starting one year after purchase, Cloud access and maintenance updates are $4.95/yr.

‚Äîand any of the myriad other editors look much better: they all just use a sync engine I already use and like, and they don‚Äôt have annual fees for a service I don‚Äôt care about.

That goes double when you consider that I‚Äôll often do different phases of drafting a given post in different editors, depending on the kind of content and what I‚Äôm doing with it. For example, I often use Caret for drafting technical blog posts, but at times I‚Äôll switch over to using Sublime Text, Atom, or VS Code for working on the details of a given code snippet. If I‚Äôm using Write‚Äôs custom sync solution, my documents don‚Äôt exist in a normal folder on my machine, so they aren‚Äôt available for that kind of easy switching and editing. Double that again because it also means I don‚Äôt have access to the content on my iPad‚Äîwhere I often use Ulysses, Editorial, 1Writer, or Byword to work on posts when I‚Äôm away from my Mac. There are no upsides for me, as far as I can tell, to using their sync system.

I put this in the ‚Äújust okay‚Äù section, however, because I can imagine that it might be nice for someone who‚Äôs not already invested in an existing sync solution. Whether or not there are enough of those people out there to support the business model‚ÄîI suspect not‚Äîis a separate question to whether it‚Äôs good or bad for users in a direct sense. Again: the custom sync system works well; I just don‚Äôt know whether it‚Äôs necessary (or worth the development time that had to be spent on it).

As for the business model on the whole: I‚Äôm not at all opposed to paying for good apps on an ongoing basis. To the contrary, I actually embrace it: as a software developer myself, I recognize that there are few (if any) other sustainable business models. However, the application needs to be pretty amazing to get me to buy it in the first place, still less to justify a recurring purchase.

The Bad

Sad to say, from my perspective‚Äîto be clear, as a long-time Mac user with very high standards for my writing tools‚Äîthis isn‚Äôt an amazing app. In fact, on macOS, it‚Äôs actually a bad app in many ways.

Non-native UI

First, Write‚Äôs UI looks and behaves like a Windows app. It‚Äôs built on Qt, which does support native(-looking) widgets, but the developers chose not to use them ‚Äì I assume in the interest of speed of development. If you‚Äôre on Windows, that‚Äôs fine. But this app will never look remotely native on macOS,4 and given the plethora of other really high quality writing apps on macOS‚Äîsome of them with their own publication options!‚Äîthere‚Äôs just no reason why you would pick this over one of those at that most basic level.

Two examples should illustrate how painfully non-native this app is visually. First, note the window action buttons in the upper right:

These are Windows window action buttons; the normal Mac action buttons simply don‚Äôt exist! Similarly, there‚Äôs a slide-out menu that appears when you tap the hamburger in the top left:

This is a reasonably nice, though not totally native-feeling, way of tackling the menu problem‚Ä¶ on Windows. On Mac, it‚Äôs just duplicating the functionality of the normal menubar. And when I say duplicating, I mean it exactly: those menus are the same as the ones the app puts in the real menubar; there‚Äôs no reason for them to appear within the body of the app, other than that the app isn‚Äôt designed to work without them.

Right-click behavior is strange: instead of the normal Mac (or even Windows!) menu, they‚Äôve supplied their own, and it‚Äôs actually its own little modal window, not a menu at all:

I definitely see the utility of the little modal, but most other apps I‚Äôve seen with similar approaches do it on highlighting some text. That way they can leave the normal right-click menu in place, which helps keep the user comfortable in their normal workflows. That‚Äôs going to be particularly annoying if you happen to make heavy use of macOS‚Äôs services menu‚ÄîI don‚Äôt use it often, but when I want it, I want it.

Keyboard shortcuts

Similarly, a number of standard keyboard shortcuts don‚Äôt work the same way, or don‚Äôt work at all, in Write! as they do in native Mac apps. Navigation controls aren‚Äôt quite right: ‚å•‚Üí jumps to the start of the next word instead of the end of the current word; ‚å•‚Üê doesn‚Äôt skip over punctuation; both stop on e.g.¬†apostrophes in Write! Other common shortcuts are bound to the wrong things: Shift‚å•-, for example, increases heading size instead of inserting an em dash. ‚åòDelete doesn‚Äôt do anything; neither do ^‚åòSpace, (normally used for bringing up the special-character selector) or my beloved ^K (‚Äúkill to end of line‚Äù) or ^T (‚Äútranspose characters around cursor) combos.5 I imagine the list is longer; that‚Äôs just what I noticed in the course of writing this review!

Most egregiously, Write! steals the keyboard shortcut ‚åò`, normally used to switch between windows on macOS, to focus itself. Failing to implement and indeed overriding text input commands is one (very bad) thing; this is another kind of failure entirely. Apps should never override core system behavior with their shortcuts! A lot of apps get some of those more obscure ones wrong, sadly, but proper use of Core Text is a must for a native app in my book‚Äîand missing those super common ones is a big no-no. Markdown support

The app claims Markdown support, and it sort of has it. But the goal is clearly to have a rich-text editing experience which can translate Markdown into whatever the underlying format is on the fly, and then export it back out when desired‚Äînot to be a Markdown writing application. You can see direct evidence that this is their approach by writing in Markdown and e.g.¬†creating italics with * characters. When you view the exported Markdown, it‚Äôll be using _ characters instead. Other little things flag it up equally: Markdown items don‚Äôt get converted to their rich text implementations unless you add a space or some punctuation after typing them; if you go back and wrap words in link syntax, for example, or try to make it bold with a pair of *s, it won‚Äôt be converted at all. The export still works fine in that case,6 but it certainly doesn‚Äôt come off well for the writing experience in the app, inconsistent as it is.

It also doesn‚Äôt support Markdown itself fully or properly. Inline backtick characters (`) don‚Äôt generate inline code snippets. Instead, they generate standalone code blocks, as if using the usual four-space-indent or triple-backtick markers for code blocks in the actual Markdown spec and as supported in other apps. Nor can I find a way to insert hrules/divisions with triple-stars or triple-dashes.

Other nits

There are a few other small but significant problems as well. One is related to the business model: you actually have to sign in to start using the app. Granted all my positive comments about subscriptions above, it‚Äôs still the case that needing to sign in to a writing app (especially just to use the app for local documents!) is a non-starter for me. As with so many of the other negatives I noted, this is a compromise that I don‚Äôt need to make, because the other alternatives don‚Äôt force it on me.

There are also a bunch of basically rough edges. Pasting with ‚åòV does indeed paste the text‚Ä¶ and scrolls you to the top of the current document every time. A number of times, the selection of a given option failed: it simply wouldn‚Äôt stick. Other times, especially when selecting the default text theme, cursor selection seemed broken. I‚Äôm not sure whether those are problems with the Qt engine, the implementation, or some of both, but again: not a good look, especially in a crowded market. Right-clicking, beyond the problems mentioned above, also just wouldn‚Äôt work consistently. Sometimes I would right-click and the menu would close immediately so you couldn‚Äôt take any actions in it at all‚Äîprobably a result of using a modal instead of a normal menu there. Regardless of the reason, it was frustrating.

Last but not least, the app is unsigned, which means that it literally won‚Äôt open by default on macOS as of a few versions back. Users can certainly get around that, but they shouldn‚Äôt have to: there‚Äôs no excuse for not signing a paid app for macOS (or Windows! But I‚Äôm not sure what its status is there) in 2017.

Conclusion

This is an interesting approach for an editor. Trying to build a truly cross-platform app, and especially one that isn‚Äôt using web technologies like Electron, is an admirable goal‚Äîin fact, it‚Äôs one that I may dare to tackle myself at some point. Cross-platform UI is also a very hard problem, and unfortunately this app makes clear just how difficult it is by falling down so often on really important details. In reality, the only way to do it well is to write all your core business logic in a way you can share and then supply actually-native user interfaces. Anything else will inevitably feel out of place at best.

As a result, Write! is deeply compromised as a Mac app, to the extent that I simply cannot recommend it for Mac users. If you‚Äôre on a Mac, you should look instead at Ulysses, Byword, and Caret. All of them feel much more native, and though they have different strengths and weaknesses, they‚Äôre all native (or mostly-very-effectively native-acting, in Caret‚Äôs case) apps. That doesn‚Äôt mean Write! is bad; it just means it‚Äôs not worth your time (a) if you‚Äôre on a Mac or (b) if you really care about standard Markdown behaviors.

As noted, though, the developers got some important parts of this very right: the app performs well, it looks decent on Windows, and their sync engine seems incredibly solid. Accordingly, if you‚Äôre on Windows, and don‚Äôt already have a particular commitment to Markdown proper, I might even cautiously recommend it‚Äîas a replacement for something like the old LiveWriter app, for example. The biggest hesitation I‚Äôd have there is the business model‚Äîand, as noted above, I‚Äôm not opposed in principle to subscription models for good apps; but I‚Äôm not really sure what the value proposition here is.
